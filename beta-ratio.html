Understanding Filter Efficiency and Beta Ratios

By: Jeremy Wright, NORIA Corporation

It is a well-established fact that the use of clean oil increases the longevity of equipment and promotes machine reliability. Equipment running with clean oil experiences fewer breakdowns which can often be costly. Many companies have addressed their need for cleaner oil by purchasing and operating expensive filtration equipment. While this may be well-intended, I cannot begin to tell you the number of in-plant filtration skids I have seen that have been running in bypass mode and have been for months if not years. Some equipment has been abandoned and sits in a corner while still others display the need for periodic filter changes with the most recent recorded change being 4 years ago.

Besides the commitment necessary to keep filtration running effectively, there is a level of understanding regarding lubricant filtration that is often lacking. First and foremost, the operator of do-it-yourself filtration must understand filter classification. Using a nominal filter when an absolute beta-rated filter is required is one example of end users throwing good money after bad. The end must also have some accurate way to measure the result of their efforts and be aware of the possible effects on the integrity of the oil being filtered.

For most who endeavor to perform do-it-yourself filtration, the questions are: how clean is clean enough, how do I know when I’m there, and is it possible to over filter lubricating oil?

The Importance of Beta Ratios

In general, the lower the ISO cleanliness code (thus the cleaner the oil), the greater is the opportunity to extend the equipment life and improve machine reliability. Depending on the type and composition of the oil and the system in which it operates, there can often be a point of diminishing returns. For example, hydraulic oil in a hydraulic system usually needs to be much cleaner than heavy gear oil operating in a large gear box. So why would you want to clean both to the same degree of cleanliness?

Some consider a “once-through” filtration adequate to achieve the necessary cleanliness. This is often provided by the lubricant supplier at the time of delivery or by the end user at the point of introducing the lubricant into the equipment. While this will improve the cleanliness of the oil, it is usually insufficient to reduce the cleanliness of new oil from ISO 21/19/17 to something like a requirement such as ISO 16/14/12 for critical systems.

Conclusion

Achieving and maintaining the appropriate cleanliness levels in lubricants is critical for optimal machine performance and longevity. Understanding beta ratios and implementing the proper filtration solutions is a vital part of this process. Clean oil is key to ensuring reliability, reducing maintenance costs, and preventing unexpected equipment failures.
